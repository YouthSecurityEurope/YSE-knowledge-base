    Google Workspace Data Classification Labels

    Audience: All YSE staff and volunteers
    Last updated: June 2026

    Overview

    Youth Security Europe (YSE) handles various types of information, from public announcements to sensitive personal data. To ensure data is handled securely, we use Google Workspace Data Classification Labels.

    Information assets are classified into three categories: Public, Internal, and Confidential. Members must handle data according to the specific requirements of its classification level.

    How to Apply Labels in Google Workspace

    (Please provide the following screenshots so they can be added to this section:) - [Screenshot 1: The label badge next to the document title in Google Docs/Sheets/Slides, or File > Labels menu] - [Screenshot 2: The label selection menu showing the Public, Internal, and Confidential options] - [Screenshot 3: Where the label is visible in the Google Drive file list or file details pane]

    You can apply classification labels directly from within a Google Doc, Sheet, or Slide, or from Google Drive:

    1. Inside a Document: Click on the label badge next to the document title at the top left, or go to File > Labels.
    2. Select the Label: Choose the appropriate label (Public, Internal, or Confidential) from the menu based on the document's content.
    3. In Google Drive: You can view and manage labels in the file details pane on the right side of your Drive view.

    Default Classification

    Internal is the default classification for most work. If you are unsure, start with Internal.

    Classification Levels

    Level 1: Public

    Definition: Information intended for public consumption or release. Disclosure causes no harm to the organization or individuals.

    Examples: Press releases, job postings, published annual reports, marketing materials on the website.

    Allowed Actions: - Sharing freely with anyone, inside or outside the organization. - Posting on social media or the public website. - Storing on personal devices without encryption.

    Disallowed Actions: - Modifying content without authorization (integrity protection). - Using outdated or retracted versions for official communication.

    Level 2: Internal

    Definition: Information strictly for internal operations. This is the default classification for most work. Unauthorized disclosure could cause confusion, reputational embarrassment, or loss of credibility.

    Examples: Internal memos, unfinished drafts, handbooks, standard operating procedures, internal meeting notes.

    Allowed Actions: - Sharing with any active member of the organization. - Storing on approved cloud drives (Google Workspace Shared Drives).

    Disallowed Actions: - Posting publicly (social media, websites, public forums). - Sending to personal email addresses or storing on personal cloud drives (e.g., personal Dropbox/iCloud). - Forwarding to external parties without a specific operational need. Personal initiatives cannot be considered operational needs in any circumstances.

    Level 3: Confidential

    Definition: Highly sensitive information where unauthorized access could result in significant financial loss, legal liability, physical harm, or severe reputational damage. Access is restricted strictly to "Need-to-Know."

    Ownership: It is mandatory to define the owner of each document classified as Confidential; the default owner is the user who created the document.

    Examples: Personally Identifiable Information (PII), donor lists, financial bank records, passwords/private keys, legal/strategy documents, private communications with external sources.

    Allowed Actions: - Accessing only on devices with Full Disk Encryption enabled. - Sharing only with specific individuals who require the data to perform their job. The access for each individual must be approved by the owner of the document. - Discussing in private environments only (avoiding public spaces or open offices where screens are visible). - Deleting or shredding immediately when no longer needed.

    Disallowed Actions: - Sending via standard, unencrypted email. - Downloading to local storage (hard drives, USBs) unless absolutely necessary and encrypted. - Sharing credentials via chat applications (e.g., Slack, Teams, WhatsApp). - Sharing content of such documents in cloud-based systems that do not guarantee not to store data, such as AI chatbots or assistants. - Printing on shared printers where the document might sit unattended.

    Confidential Data Requires Care

    Always double-check permissions when handling Confidential data to ensure only authorized individuals have access.
